\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\textbf{Datasets.}
\label{sec:datasets}
We evaluate our framework on the \textit{DiDi GAIA Open Dataset}. The dataset contains high-resolution GPS trajectories and trip dispatch logs from November 2016, covering approximately 7.06 million completed trips and over 1.09 billion GPS pings. We use 9 November 2016, a representative weekday, as the primary evaluation instance, which includes 36,799 workers and 224,219 tasks in the core urban area around Chengdu. Each unique driver is modeled as a worker, with their first GPS ping used to determine the online time and initial location, and an 8-hour shift duration is applied. Each completed trip is converted into one task, where the dispatch timestamp is used as the release time, the billing-completion timestamp is used as the expiry deadline, and the recorded fare is used as the revenue signal. Distances are computed using Manhattan distance under a flat-earth projection, and travel time is estimated using a constant urban speed of 30,km/h.

To test whether the observed strategy behavior generalizes beyond dense ride-hailing scenarios, we also evaluate on the \textit{Gowalla} location-based social network dataset~\cite{cho2011friendship}. We focus on the Austin, Texas region during September 2010, which provides the densest usable check-in cluster in the dataset. Since raw LBSN check-ins are much sparser than ride-hailing requests, we apply temporal compression by projecting all check-ins onto a single 24-hour reference window while preserving their wall-clock times and spatial distribution. Each unique user-day pair is treated as one worker, and each check-in is converted into one task with a 30-minute expiry window. Because Gowalla does not contain trip destinations or fares, drop-off locations and revenues are generated using distance-based proxies. After compression and worker downsampling, the Gowalla evaluation instance contains 8,758 workers and 43,788 tasks, providing a sparser and structurally different environment for testing the robustness of assignment strategies.

\textbf{Implementation Details.}
\label{sec:framework}
We implement a dataset-agnostic discrete event simulator (DES) driven by a min-heap priority queue ordered by Unix timestamps, ensuring that all events are processed chronologically without artificial batching or future lookahead, consistent with the instantaneous matching constraint in online spatial crowdsourcing~\cite{tong2019survey}. The simulator supports worker release, task release, task completion, and task expiration events: newly available workers trigger a \textsc{FreeWorker} handler to search for pending tasks, newly released tasks trigger a \textsc{NewTask} handler to search for available workers, completed tasks return workers to the candidate pool, and expired tasks are removed from the pending pool. Each assignment strategy is implemented through the same pair of handlers, $(\textsc{NewTask}, \textsc{FreeWorker})$, which receive the current system state and return at most one assignment per event. Available workers are maintained in an incrementally updated $k$-d tree spatial index based on their current locations, enabling efficient $k$-nearest-neighbour queries without rebuilding the full index at every event. Distances are computed using Manhattan distance under a flat-earth equirectangular projection, and travel times assume a constant average urban speed of 30,km/h. Each candidate pair is filtered by two feasibility constraints: the worker must reach the pickup location before the task deadline, i.e., $t_{\text{now}} + d_{\text{pick}}/v \leq e_j$, and must complete the trip before the worker's shift deadline, i.e., $t_{\text{now}} + (d_{\text{pick}} + d_{\text{drop}})/v \leq t_{\text{deadline}}^w$. Candidate assignments violating either condition are rejected before scoring, which prevents infeasible matches without introducing additional penalty terms.

\textbf{Baselines.}
We compare our approach with representative online, fairness-aware, randomized, and batch-based assignment baselines. Greedy minimizes pickup distance only by setting $\lambda_1,\lambda_2=0$, while Least Allocated Worker First (LAF) assigns tasks to workers with the fewest completed tasks as a pure fairness baseline. EWMA-Only uses only the proposed EWMA idle-time fairness signal without utility or starvation terms, and Fixed-Composite applies a static grid-searched weight configuration of our composite framework. BiRanking (BRK) is a randomized ranking-based policy inspired by \cite{[CITATION]}, which greedily matches feasible workers and tasks according to fixed random ranks. FATP-ANN is an existing fairness-aware online assignment method \cite{CR-08}. TSGF Sampling adapts the Two-Sided Group Fairness framework \cite{[CITATION]} by randomly selecting operator-profit, worker-fairness, or task-fairness heuristics at each dispatch event. Cost-Balancing (CB) \cite{[CITATION]} and MMD-Batch (MMD-BB) \cite{CR-11} are online batching baselines that defer decisions to balance matching cost or minimize worst-case delay. Discrete Review LP \cite{[CITATION]} periodically solves a global bipartite assignment problem via the Hungarian algorithm, while ONRTA-RT and ONRTA-OP \cite{[CITATION]} represent randomized-threshold and optimal-threshold variants of the Online Non-Rejection Task Assignment policy.

\textbf{Evaluation Metrics.}
To evaluate how well the proposed composite assignment framework navigates the tripartite trade-off among efficiency, fairness, and starvation prevention, we report metrics covering platform performance, worker equity, service reliability, and computational cost. Platform efficiency is measured by the \textit{Task Assignment Ratio} (TAR), defined as the fraction of successfully matched requests over all task arrivals ($|M|/|\mathcal{T}|$), together with \textit{Total Platform Revenue} ($k$), which reflects the economic viability of the assignment strategy. Supply-side equity is evaluated using Jain's Fairness Index (JFI) \cite{JFI-Original}, computed over the vector of cumulative task counts $\mathbf{x}$ across active workers:
$JFI(\mathbf{x}) = \left.{(\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i)^2} \middle/n {\sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i^2} \right.$,
where $n$ is the number of workers and a value closer to 1.0 indicates a more equitable task distribution. Demand-side reliability is captured by \textit{Mean Wait Time}, which measures the average interval from task release $r_j$ to worker pickup, and \textit{Average Pickup Distance}, which measures the mean empty travel distance required for workers to reach pickup locations. Together, these metrics indicate whether a strategy achieves responsiveness through efficient spatial matching or at the cost of longer detours. Finally, we report \textit{Peak Backlog}, defined as the maximum number of simultaneously unassigned tasks during a simulation day, to assess resilience to demand spikes, and \textit{Wall-clock Runtime} to evaluate whether the method remains computationally practical for city-scale deployment.

\textbf{Throughput.}
Across all strategies, the Task Assignment Ratio is near-constant at TAR $\approx$ 0.863 on DiDi and $\approx$ 0.999 on Gowalla (Tables~\ref{tab:didi_results}--\ref{tab:gowalla_results}).
The near-invariance reflects that the matching bottleneck is task expiry and worker availability, not the assignment rule — almost every task that can feasibly be matched is matched.
This removes TAR as a differentiating metric and shifts the analysis to the three-way trade-off between fairness, latency, and compute.
The one exception is FATP-ANN (TAR $=$ 0.599 on DiDi), which loses 26\% of tasks due to threshold over-rejection at city scale; its results are shown in parentheses and excluded from the column-best comparison.

\textbf{Worker fairness.}
k-NLF achieves JFI $=$ 0.649 on DiDi, a 9.3\% improvement over Greedy (0.594), with JFI (earnings) rising from 0.613 to 0.669 ($+$9.1\%).
On Gowalla the absolute gains are larger — JFI 0.566 $\to$ 0.672 ($+$18.7\%) — confirming that the local task-count signal provides consistent fairness improvements across both dataset types.
LTF achieves the highest raw fairness (JFI $=$ 0.705 DiDi, 0.894 Gowalla), but does so by globally sorting workers on task count, incurring an average wait of 9.63\,min on DiDi — 3.7$\times$ worse than Greedy (2.62\,min) and 2.8$\times$ worse than k-NLF (3.45\,min).
This confirms that unconstrained global fairness is achievable but operationally unacceptable at city scale.
BiRanking falls \emph{below} the Greedy baseline on both datasets (DiDi: JFI $=$ 0.592 $<$ 0.594; Gowalla: 0.360 $\ll$ 0.566), demonstrating that randomised ranking simultaneously degrades both fairness and throughput.

Composite achieves JFI (tasks) $=$ 0.589 on DiDi, marginally below Greedy, because its EWMA idle-time signal optimises temporal participation equity rather than snapshot task counts.
Its JFI rate (0.870) nonetheless exceeds Greedy (0.864), and its average wait (3.20\,min) is lower than k-NLF (3.45\,min), indicating that the two proposed methods are complementary: k-NLF leads on task-count equity, Composite on temporal participation equity and task latency.
The ablation in \S\ref{sec:ablation} characterises this trade-off further.

\textbf{Task latency.}
Discrete Review LP achieves the lowest average wait on both datasets (1.64\,min DiDi, 2.72\,min Gowalla), consistent with its batch-deferral design: by holding arrivals until a globally optimal bipartite assignment is solved, it reduces unnecessary detours.
This benefit comes, however, at a 133$\times$ runtime cost over k-NLF (5{,}060\,s vs.\ 38\,s on DiDi), rendering it impractical for real-time deployment.
Among online strategies, Greedy achieves low wait (2.62\,min) by always dispatching the nearest worker, but sacrifices worker fairness to do so.

\textbf{Platform revenue.}
Revenue is effectively flat across all valid strategies, ranging from 2{,}749.8–2{,}765.8\,k\$ on DiDi — a spread of just 0.06\%.
The same pattern holds on Gowalla (294.5–297.7\,k\$, 1.1\% spread).
This confirms that the fairness improvements delivered by k-NLF and Composite come at zero cost to platform revenue.
The only outlier is FATP-ANN (2{,}206.9\,k\$, $-$20\%), entirely attributable to its task dropout.

\textbf{Computational cost.}
k-NLF completes the full DiDi day in 38\,s — 9.4$\times$ faster than Greedy (357\,s) — because its $k{=}15$ candidate pool caps the per-event feasibility scan, whereas Greedy must traverse the k-d tree until it finds the nearest \emph{feasible} worker, a search whose depth grows with the infeasible-worker fraction.
This inversion — a fairness-aware method outperforming a throughput-only baseline in wall-clock runtime — is a key practical property of the proposed approach.
ONRTA-RT (416\,s) and BiRanking (434\,s) are both slower than Greedy despite achieving lower fairness, further demonstrating that algorithmic complexity and fairness quality are not in a simple trade-off.

\textbf{Cross-dataset consistency.}
Strategy rankings on Gowalla mirror DiDi: k-NLF leads among online $O(k)$ methods on JFI, LTF achieves maximum JFI at prohibitive wait cost, BiRanking falls below the Greedy baseline, and FATP-ANN remains degenerate.
This stability across two structurally different platforms — high-density ride-hailing versus sparse LBSN check-ins, with all hyperparameters held fixed at their DiDi-tuned values — supports the generalisability of the proposed approach.
